Here are the common pain points, backed by research:

**Prediction accuracy**
Apps are most accurate for very regular cycles, but the people who most need an app are those with irregular cycles — and many apps don't let users explain anomalies (illness, stress, medication changes) that throw off the algorithm. Many calendar-based apps still assume ovulation on day 14, which is inaccurate for the majority of users.

**Data privacy**
Apps collect highly intimate data — sexual activity, hormone levels, contraception use — and investigations have revealed apps sharing this data with third parties including advertisers, data brokers, and tech giants like Facebook and Google. A California jury found Meta had illegally collected reproductive health data from Flo users without knowledge or consent.

**UX & design**
Users reacted negatively to pink, flowery iconography ("shrink it and pink it") and preferred neutral, functional displays. They also wanted the ability to export data to other health tools, and for the app to recognize that tracking needs change over time.

**Inconsistent data entry**
About 38% of users frequently forget to enter data, and most only log during their period rather than throughout the cycle — reducing the quality of predictions.

**Feature bloat / missing customization**
Real user reviews highlight being unable to hide irrelevant tracking categories, features disappearing after updates, and lacking the ability to add free-form notes to specific days.

**Paywalls**
Key features (detailed analytics, predictions, cycle history) are commonly gated behind subscriptions, frustrating users who want functional core tracking for free.
